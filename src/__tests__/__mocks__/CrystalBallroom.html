Crystal Facts

Opened in 1914 as Cotillion Hall, the dance hall was rechristened in 1950 as the Crystal Ballroom. It wasn’t until 1997 that McMenamins took stewardship of the legendary building.
After being shut down by the city in 1968 due to “code violations,” the Crystal was dark for nearly 30 years, despite failed attempts to revive it. Then, in 1997, McMenamins Crystal Ballroom was born.
Heard about the Crystal’s “famous floating dance floor”? It's ball bearing and rocker design, first patented in 1905 by Charles R. Hunt, brought a whole new sensation to Portland dancing.
An early 1900s guest said of the dance hall’s floating floor that it made “a good dancer out of a bad one.” Another recalled that it made dancing “almost effortless.”
The Crystal’s famous “floating” dance floor’s bounce factor could originally be adjusted using a racheted gear. Appropriate to each dance, the floor could be made rigid or wide open.
The Crystal is said to have a strong “sense of place,” a phenomenon defined as an energy that affects the quality of the experience and its outcomes for the group that is gathered within.
Just a few of the performers who have graced the Crystal stage: Ike & Tina Turner, the Grateful Dead, Patti Smith, James Brown, Wilco, B.B. King, Sleater-Kinney, Motorhead -- and the list goes on….
The Crystal’s vibrant energy attracted Mike McMenamin for years. After its purchase, Mike said, “It’s one of those places that deserves to live forever [but] we just have custody for awhile.”
During the 1950s, the Crystal might have hosted a high-society old-time dance, a sorority pajama party, a Gypsy wake, and James Brown, all in one week.
Gary Ewing was the Crystal’s legendary light show artist in the late ‘60s, filling the space with transcendent color and images in “harmonic totality” with the music.
“My ambition is to fill a volume, that is a ballroom, with color the way a glass is filled with water. The people would be dancing in color, covered with color.” – Gary Ewing, light show artist, 1967
The building’s first owner, Montrose Ringler, is credited with bringing the new-fangled sport of “basketball” to Portland.
During the Grateful Dead’s 1968 set at the Crystal, the power cut out mid-song. Strangely, that track is the one that ended up on Anthem of the Sun, one of Rolling Stone’s “500 greatest albums.”
Early 1910s reformers (including Lola Baldwin, namesake of Lola’s Room) sought to curb the vices of dancing and drinking. Headlines included DANCEHALL HELD EVIL and HUGGING BARRED AT PUBLIC DANCES.
Portland’s Lola Baldwin, the nation’s first policewoman, sought to curb the evils of dancing, drinking and loose morals in the 1910s. Naturally, we named a bar for her. Raise a glass at Lola’s Room.
At one 1967 show at the Crystal, guests saw The Warlocks, Mr. Lucky & The Gamblers and The Weeds before headliners Buffalo Springfield. Ticket cost? $2.25 for a single, $4 for a couple.
In the late ‘50s and early ‘60s, colorful Gypsy wakes were held at the Crystal as part of that populations’ funereal traditions, complete with food, beer, music, dancing and more.
One long-running rumor is that, during a Crystal show in 1965, Little Richard fired his guitarist in a fit of pique and ego. That guitarist, it is said, was none other than Jimi Hendrix.
